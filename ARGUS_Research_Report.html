Abstract

ARGUS (Autonomous Research & Governance Utility System) is a full-stack technology intelligence and strategic forecasting platform designed to autonomously ingest, analyze, and visualize emerging technology signals across defense-relevant domains. The system integrates real-time data feeds from arXiv, CrossRef, PatentsView, and RSS news sources into a unified SQLite intelligence database. Ingested data undergoes multi-stage processing through a pipeline comprising keyword-based signal extraction, optional LLM-augmented semantic analysis (via OpenAI GPT-4o-mini), automated Technology Readiness Level (TRL) estimation, source trust scoring, and fuzzy deduplication.

The platform implements seven distinct analytical engines: a Breakout Detection Engine that identifies velocity spikes and cluster surges; an Emergence Detector combining publication acceleration, vocabulary novelty, and cross-domain convergence; a Momentum Engine computing weighted innovation scores; a Dependency Discovery Engine constructing co-occurrence graphs; a Graph Pruning Engine with temporal decay; a deterministic Horizon Engine computing non-linear temporal compression; and a Monte Carlo Scenario Engine with seeded PRNG for reproducible probabilistic forecasting.

The frontend presents 16 specialized dashboard views organized into a four-section Intelligence Cycle information architecture, rendered through a warm-cream editorial design system. The system implements a governance accountability framework with a Decision Ledger, cryptographic export signing (SHA-256), and role-based access control.

Chapter 1Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

Governments, defense agencies, and strategic research institutions face an accelerating challenge: the velocity of emerging technology development now outpaces institutional capacity to detect, assess, and respond to critical signals. A single academic paper on arXiv can describe a breakthrough in quantum key distribution that, within 18 months, reshapes the strategic posture of national cybersecurity infrastructure. Patent filings from semiconductor foundries signal shifts in supply chain dependencies affecting defense procurement timelines months before they become visible in policy forums.

The core problem is information overload combined with analytical latency. Existing approaches rely on manual scanning by domain experts who cannot monitor the full breadth of academic publications, patent databases, news feeds, and industry reports across all relevant technology clusters simultaneously. By the time a traditional horizon scanning exercise produces a report, the signal landscape has already shifted.

1.2 Motivation

  1. Fragmented Data Sources: Technology intelligence data is dispersed across arXiv (preprints), CrossRef (journals), USPTO (patents), and defense/technology news outlets. No single platform unifies these into a coherent signal stream.
  2. Absence of Automated TRL Estimation: While the Technology Readiness Level framework is universally adopted in defense procurement, TRL assignment remains a subjective, expert-driven process.
  3. Static Forecasting Models: Traditional horizon scanning produces point-in-time reports. Strategic decision-makers need continuous, dynamic models that update as new signals arrive.
  4. Governance Accountability Deficit: When strategic technology decisions are made (or not made), there is rarely a cryptographically verifiable audit trail linking the decision to evidence state.

1.3 Objectives

  1. Autonomous Data Ingestion: Build a multi-source ingestion pipeline fetching research papers, patents, and news articles from external APIs with zero manual intervention.
  2. Intelligent Signal Extraction: Develop a hybrid extraction pipeline combining keyword-based detection with optional LLM-augmented semantic analysis.
  3. Multi-Algorithm Analysis: Implement seven analytical engines for breakout detection, emergence detection, momentum scoring, dependency graph construction, and probabilistic horizon forecasting.
  4. Institutional Governance: Create a decision accountability framework with cryptographic integrity.
  5. Editorial-Grade Visualization: Design a 16-view dashboard communicating institutional intelligence with clarity and authority.

Chapter 2Literature Review

2.1 Technology Intelligence Systems

Technology intelligence (TI) has evolved from manual expert-driven scanning processes to semi-automated systems. Kerr et al. (2006) define TI as "the capture and delivery of technological information as part of the process whereby an organization develops awareness of technological threats and opportunities." Early systems such as the NATO Technology Watch relied on manual report compilation. More recent approaches, including DARPA's Foresight programs, employ NLP and machine learning to accelerate signal detection.

2.2 Horizon Scanning Methodologies

Horizon scanning is a systematic examination of potential threats, opportunities, and likely future developments (Government Office for Science, UK, 2017). Traditional methods include Delphi panels, STEEP analysis, and scenario planning workshops. The ARGUS Horizon Engine departs from these qualitative approaches by implementing a purely deterministic, mathematically auditable model with non-linear impact amplification.

2.3 Technology Readiness Levels (TRL)

The TRL framework, developed by NASA (Mankins, 1995), provides a standardized scale from TRL 1 (basic principles observed) to TRL 9 (actual system proven in operational environment). The European Commission adopted TRL as part of Horizon 2020. ARGUS implements automated TRL estimation via regex-based language signal detection mapping nine distinct patterns to TRL levels.

2.4 Knowledge Graphs in Technology Forecasting

Knowledge graphs have been increasingly adopted for technology forecasting. Chen et al. (2019) demonstrated that co-occurrence networks from patent data can predict technology convergence patterns 3–5 years ahead. ARGUS implements an automated Dependency Discovery Engine constructing co-occurrence graphs with Jaccard-like similarity metrics.

2.5 Monte Carlo Methods in Strategic Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation has been applied to strategic planning in defense contexts since the 1940s (Metropolis & Ulam, 1949). ARGUS implements a 200-run Monte Carlo simulation using a seeded Mulberry32 PRNG ensuring audit reproducibility.

2.6 Research Gap

Research Gap Identified

No existing system integrates multi-source autonomous ingestion, LLM-augmented semantic analysis with heuristic fallback, seven analytical engines on a shared database, a governance accountability framework with cryptographic signing, and an editorial-grade frontend for defense audiences — into a single, unified platform. ARGUS fills this gap.

Chapter 4Database Design

4.1 Schema Design Philosophy

  1. Append-Only Staging: Raw source data is never modified after insertion. The raw_sources table serves as an immutable audit log.
  2. Upsert Semantics: The signals table uses INSERT OR IGNORE for initial seeding and explicit UPDATE for enrichment.
  3. Denormalized for Speed: Fields like score_history, priority_drivers, and impact_domains are stored as JSON strings, trading storage for read performance.

4.3 Index Strategy

The schema includes 28 production indexes optimized for high-frequency query patterns:

  • Signals: Composite index on (technology, cluster); descending on priority_score; index on created_at DESC
  • Alerts: Composite on (acknowledged, created_at DESC) for "unacknowledged, newest first" queries
  • Raw Sources: Composite on (fetcher, processed) for pipeline staging queries
  • Momentum: Composite on (technology, year) for time-series aggregation

Chapter 6Analytical Engines

ARGUS implements seven distinct analytical engines, each operating on the shared SQLite database:

Core Signal Extraction

26-keyword dictionary across 7 clusters with alias matching and TRL estimation.

Core Semantic Signal (LLM)

GPT-4o-mini semantic analysis with heuristic fallback for API-free operation.

Analysis Breakout Detection

Four algorithms: velocity spikes, breakout candidates, momentum acceleration, cluster surges.

Analysis Emergence Detector

Three-factor composite: publication acceleration (40%), vocabulary novelty (30%), convergence (30%).

Analysis Momentum Engine

Weighted scoring: research (35%), patents (25%), citations (20%), funding (10%), startups (10%).

Graph Dependency Discovery

Builds co-occurrence graphs from corpus with Jaccard-like edge weighting.

Graph Graph Pruning

Four-rule cleanup: threshold, temporal decay, minimum strength, per-node edge limits.

Forecast Horizon Engine

Deterministic temporal compression with non-linear impact and cascade amplification.

Forecast Monte Carlo Scenario

200-run simulation with Mulberry32 seeded PRNG for reproducible probabilistic forecasting.

6.2 Breakout Detection Engine

Algorithm 1: Velocity Spike Detection

Computes ratio of recent velocity to historical average. Severity thresholds:

RatioSeverity
> 1.8CRITICAL
> 1.5WARNING
> 1.3WATCH

Algorithm 2: Breakout Candidate Identification

Identifies signals exhibiting: recent velocity > 0.05, TRL ≥ 5, priority ≥ 80. Score = recentVelocity × 100 × TRL. Always classified as CRITICAL.

Algorithm 3: Momentum Acceleration

Compares first-half vs. second-half average velocity. Triggers at ratio > 1.5 with minimum 5 data points.

Algorithm 4: Cluster Surge Detection

Aggregates per-cluster velocities. Triggers when surgeRatio > 0.5 AND avgVelocity > 0.04.

6.3 Emergence Detector

Emergence Composite Score early_signal_score = publication_acceleration × 0.4 + vocabulary_novelty × 0.3 + cross_domain_links × 0.3

Factor 1: Publication Acceleration (40%)

Weighted Year-over-Year Growth weightedAvgGrowth = Σ(growthRate[i] × (i+1)) / Σ(i+1)
Normalized to [0, 1] where >100% growth → 1.0

Factor 2: Vocabulary Novelty (30%)

TF-IDF Inspired Novelty novelty = (TF × IDF × 10) × (0.4 + 0.6 × recencyRatio)
TF = technology_mentions / total_signals
IDF = log(totalTechCount / matchingSignals)
recencyRatio = signals_last_6_months / total_signals

Factor 3: Cross-Domain Convergence (30%)

Technologies spanning 5+ distinct domains receive maximum convergence score (1.0).

6.4 Deterministic Horizon Engine

Design Constraint

All outputs are deterministic — no stochastic or heuristic elements are permitted in the core engine. Factor weights are governance-locked and require institutional review to alter.

FactorWeightDescription
Variance Magnitude0.35|Observed − Projected| / Tolerance Band
Velocity Delta0.30Signal Velocity Δ normalized (0–1)
Dependency Exposure0.20Cross-domain exposure % (0–1)
Confidence Decay0.15Confidence drop since issuance (0–1)

Weight validation: 0.35 + 0.30 + 0.20 + 0.15 = 1.00

Non-Linear Impact Function impact(weight, factor) = weight × factor × (1 + factor²)

factor = 0.1 → multiplier ≈ 1.01 (nearly linear)
factor = 0.5 → multiplier = 1.25 (mild amplification)
factor = 0.9 → multiplier = 1.81 (strong amplification)
Horizon Computation ΔH = min(1.0, shiftFactor) × BaseHorizon
NewHorizon = max(0, 12 − ΔH)

Cascade Amplification: if variance > 0.6 AND dependency > 0.6 → shiftFactor ×= 1.25

Escalation Triggers

  1. Horizon Compression: Horizon ≤ 6 months
  2. Sustained Pressure: Allocation Pressure ≥ 0.78 for 2 consecutive cycles

7.2 Institutional State Provider

A React context provider managing two isolated state slices:

  1. Engine Slice: Purely derived from base factors + governance mutation booleans. Memoized. Recomputes at most twice per review session.
  2. Governance Slice: Owns institutional memory. Hydrates from /api/governance on mount with static fallback. Auto-posts governance events when review sessions end.

7.4 Cryptographic Export Engine

  1. Freeze: All mutable state frozen via structuredClone()
  2. Sign: Payload POSTed to /api/export for SHA-256 digest computation
  3. Package: Metadata + Payload + Signature bundled as downloadable JSON artifact
  4. Download: Browser triggers file save with timestamped filename

7.5 Intelligence Brief Generator

Automated strategic reports containing: summary metrics, top-5 breakout signals, per-cluster activity status (Hot/Active/Monitoring), ingestion statistics, and auto-generated recommendations. Exportable as formatted Markdown.

9.3 Database Persistence

Persistence Architecture

Render's default model rebuilds containers from scratch on each deploy. Without a persistent disk, SQLite data is lost. A Render Disk mounted at /app/data (1 GB) ensures the database survives redeployments. An external cron service POSTs to /api/ingest every 6 hours to maintain continuous data ingestion.

Chapter 11Current Limitations

  1. Single-Node SQLite: Does not scale horizontally. PostgreSQL migration required for >1M signals.
  2. PatentsView API Instability: Intermittent availability results in zero patent data during some cycles.
  3. LLM Dependency: Without an OpenAI API key, falls back to keyword-only extraction with reduced specificity.
  4. No Real Authentication: RBAC uses client-side role switching. Production requires OAuth2 integration.
  5. Ephemeral Database: Without Render persistent disk, data is lost on redeployment.
  6. Limited Momentum Data: Funding and startup indicators are hardcoded to zero, awaiting Crunchbase/PitchBook integration.
  7. News Signal Conversion: Dictionary optimized for academic abstracts; news uses different terminology.
  8. English Only: Research in Chinese, German, or Japanese (significant in semiconductor/quantum) is not captured.
  9. Static Base Factors: Horizon Engine factors are hardcoded rather than derived from live system state.
  10. Mock Verification: Client-side export verification returns mock true, pending server-side endpoint.

Chapter 13Conclusion

ARGUS demonstrates that a comprehensive, autonomous technology intelligence platform can be architected and deployed as a full-stack web application using modern open-source technologies. The system successfully:

  1. Ingests real data from four heterogeneous external sources, processing 180+ records per cycle with zero errors.
  2. Extracts structured signals through a hybrid pipeline combining keyword-dictionary matching with optional LLM semantic analysis, achieving a 9.4% signal extraction rate.
  3. Detects emergent technologies using a novel three-factor composite model designed to identify signals 2–3 years before mainstream breakout.
  4. Enables probabilistic forecasting through a Monte Carlo simulation engine with deterministic reproducibility.
  5. Provides institutional accountability through a governance framework with cryptographic signing and an auditable decision ledger.
  6. Presents intelligence through 16 specialized dashboard views organized in a four-section Intelligence Cycle information architecture.

The platform's limitations — primarily around data source breadth, authentication maturity, and database scalability — represent engineering constraints addressable through incremental improvement rather than architectural redesign. The core analytical engines are architecturally sound and mathematically rigorous. ARGUS represents a viable foundation for a next-generation technology intelligence platform bridging the gap between academic signal detection methodologies and the operational needs of strategic decision-makers.
